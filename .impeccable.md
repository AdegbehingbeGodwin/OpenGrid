## Design Context

### Users
OpenGrid serves both technical and general audiences. It should work for developers integrating the API, researchers and civic users exploring public facility coverage, and community contributors helping improve the dataset. The interface should make the project feel legible and useful to non-technical visitors while still signaling rigor, structure, and API credibility to technical users.

### Brand Personality
OpenGrid should feel elegant, cinematic, and trustworthy. Its voice should be clear, modern, and composed rather than bureaucratic or hype-driven. The emotional goal is confidence: users should feel they are looking at serious public infrastructure data presented with care, ambition, and polish.

### Aesthetic Direction
The product should present as a world-class civic data platform with equal emphasis on light and dark modes. The visual language should combine technical precision with broad public appeal: strong typography, atmospheric but controlled contrast, premium map and data surfaces, and layouts that feel editorial rather than generic dashboard boilerplate. It should avoid looking toy-like, overly startup-generic, or excessively institutional and dull.

### Design Principles
1. Design for dual fluency: every screen should be immediately understandable to the general public while still feeling credible to technical users.
2. Make data feel consequential: use hierarchy, motion, and visual framing to make coverage, gaps, and contribution pathways feel important.
3. Treat polish as product value: typography, spacing, theming, states, and transitions should communicate seriousness and care.
4. Keep elegance disciplined: cinematic presentation is welcome, but clarity, trust, and readability take priority over spectacle.
5. Maintain parity across themes: light and dark modes should both feel fully designed, not primary and fallback.
